The Wrong Plan at the Wrong Price

El Cerrito deserves a great library—but Measure C is the wrong plan at the wrong price. It locks us into paying $100 million or more in taxes for a ground-floor commercial space that we won't even own in the BART housing project.

El Cerrito residents who love libraries came together over concerns about the high parcel tax outlined in the City's Transit Oriented Development (TOD) Plaza Library proposal and the initiative submitted by the Committee for Plaza Station Library/A New El Cerrito Library group. Our city deserves a modern library, however, we don't agree that their plan is the right one. Compelling residents into $100 million or more taxes is excessive.

The City's Plan

1A $100 million tax: City officials claim this plan is a cheap alternative to a standalone library. In reality, residents will pay at least $100 million over 30 years, and there's no guarantee it ends then.

2A forever tax that grows every year: The tax starts at 17 cents per square foot, but City Council can raise it every year without voter approval based on the higher of two different inflation measures. Over the last decade, these have averaged 4.2% per-year.

3The tax will fall on renters too: Landlords aren't going to just cover the tax increase. They are going to pass it on to their tenants with even higher rents every year.

4A developer handout disguised as a library project: Taxpayers would cover part of the BART Plaza construction costs upfront, plus tens of millions in interest payments, benefiting private developers.

5No guarantee the library will actually get built: El Cerrito residents will start paying the tax immediately. But there's no guarantee if or when a new library space will ever be built. The city's preferred location is in phase C of the BART project, which doesn't have its financing secured.

6Parking will get worse: The Plaza area is already congested. It is going to get a lot worse if the parking lots are replaced by 700 apartments. People visiting the library will have to battle with shoppers and BART commuters for parking.

7Hits seniors hardest: Forty percent of El Cerrito residents are 50 years and older. Many seniors live on fixed incomes. This measure is a substantial new annual expense—on top of rising costs for food, utilities, and healthcare—that they will have to pay forever. See the fact check.
