Measure C • Fact Check

The senior exemption does not work

Backers told seniors they would be protected from Measure C. They wrote the fine print to make qualifying nearly impossible. We don’t have to guess how it plays out — the city already uses the same exemption language in another parcel tax, and the city’s own Finance Director told a local news site that the number of seniors who have ever qualified is zero.

What you need to know: Measure C promises a senior exemption but conditions it on enrollment in one of two state programs. The first has been unfunded since 2008 and cannot process claims. The second requires the state to place a lien on your home. Neither applies to renters.

El Cerrito has used this exact exemption language before in Measure H, the Parks and Recreation parcel tax. Zero seniors have ever qualified — according to the city’s Finance Director, who confirmed it to Livable El Cerrito. There is no application form. The city’s own §9212 Impact Report acknowledges the first program is “not currently funded by the State.” Other local measures — like the school district’s Measure T — exempt any homeowner 65 and older with no state program required. The backers could have written the exemption that way. They chose not to.

1. What they said

Backers told seniors they would be exempt from the tax

Throughout the signature-gathering campaign and on their website, supporters of Measure C told El Cerrito seniors they would be protected from the tax. The official ballot question approved by the city — the one voters will actually see on their ballot — highlights “senior exemptions” as a feature of the measure, alongside audits and citizen oversight.

Here is what makes the ballot language indefensible: the city knew, before drafting it, that the exemption does not work. By the time the city finalized the ballot question, its own Finance Director had already told a local news site, on the record, that zero seniors have ever qualified for a senior exemption under the identical language in Measure H. The city also knew — because its own §9212 Impact Report says so — that the first of the two referenced state programs is not currently funded.

With that information in hand, the city still chose to tell voters the measure includes “senior exemptions” in the ballot question itself. Not a footnote. Not the fine print. The top-line pitch.

That is the pitch. What follows is what the fine print actually says.

2. What they wrote

The fine print requires enrollment in one of two state programs

Buried several pages into the measure, the actual exemption condition appears. It is not enough to be 65 and own a home. A senior must qualify for participation in one of two specific state programs — on top of meeting El Cerrito’s own requirements.

The first is the Gonzales-Deukmejian-Petris Senior Citizen Property Tax Assistance Law (also known as the HRA program). The second is the Senior Citizen Property Tax Postponement Law, administered by the State Controller’s Office. Everything turns on those two programs. One has been defunct for over 15 years. The other places a state lien on the senior’s home and excludes anyone with a reverse mortgage or more than roughly $55,000 in household income.

Neither program applies to renters. The tax is levied on buildings, so landlords pay it and pass the cost through in rent. There is no exemption path for a senior who rents.

3. Program one

The first program has been unfunded since 2008 and cannot process claims

The Gonzales-Deukmejian-Petris program was a state grant program for low-income older Californians. It has not received state funding since the 2008 budget cycle. It does not take applications. It cannot approve participation. No senior alive today can qualify under this program because the program no longer functions.

This is not a campaign talking point. It was confirmed on the record by a state analyst who administers the program. According to Livable El Cerrito’s reporting, the state’s Franchise Tax Board told them so directly:

Cristina Perfino, Analyst, California Franchise Tax Board
via Livable El Cerrito, Feb. 9
The first state program referenced in Measure C is “unfunded” and “unavailable.” It has not been funded since 2008. A bill to activate the program and reset the income limit at $30,000 per year was introduced in 2019 but did not pass.

The city agrees. Its own §9212 Impact Report, delivered to the City Council on February 19, 2026, states in plain language:

City of El Cerrito §9212 Impact Report • p. 9
The HRA program provided a direct grant to individuals over the age of 62 or who are blind or disabled and whose income is below specified levels. “The program is not currently funded by the State.”

The backers of Measure C wrote a senior exemption that depends, in part, on a state program the city itself admits does not exist anymore.

4. Program two

The second program puts a state lien on your home

The second referenced program — the Senior Citizen Property Tax Postponement Law — is real and does take applications. It also comes with conditions that eliminate most El Cerrito seniors.

According to the California State Controller’s Office, as quoted directly in the city’s §9212 report, to qualify an owner must meet all of the following:

California State Controller’s Office
via §9212 Impact Report, p. 9
• Be at least 62, or blind, or disabled
• Own and occupy the property as a principal residence
• Have total household income of $55,181 or less
• Have at least 40% equity in the property
• Not have a reverse mortgage on the property

There is one more detail the measure does not disclose and the ballot summary does not mention. The program does not actually exempt the senior from paying the tax. It postpones the payment and records a state lien against the home. The state is later repaid with interest, typically when the home is sold or the owner dies. For most El Cerrito homeowners, accepting a state lien on their home in exchange for a $331 parcel tax credit is not a reasonable trade.

An analyst from the State Controller’s Office, quoted by Livable El Cerrito, confirmed the mechanics: the state pays the property taxes on the applicant’s behalf, and a lien is placed on the property. It is not possible to confirm a homeowner qualifies unless they apply.

Statewide, fewer than one percent of eligible seniors are enrolled in the Property Tax Postponement program. There is no reason to expect enrollment in El Cerrito to look any different.

5. The track record

El Cerrito already uses this exact exemption. Zero seniors have ever qualified.

This is not a hypothetical. El Cerrito has an existing parcel tax — Measure H, the Parks and Recreation facilities tax — that uses the exact same senior exemption language tied to the same two state programs. Measure H was extended by voters in 2019 and has been on the books ever since. The city collects it every year.

Greg Lyman, the former El Cerrito City Council member who is the principal author of Measure C, confirmed in an email to Livable El Cerrito that the senior exemption language in Measure C is similar to that used in Measure H. That is a direct admission: the backers knew which exemption language they were copying, and they knew the existing version has a track record.

Livable El Cerrito reported the track record on February 10, 2026. In an email to the local news site, El Cerrito’s own Finance Director stated directly:

Crystal Reams, Finance Director and Tax Administrator, City of El Cerrito
via email, Feb. 10, 2026 — as reported in Livable El Cerrito
“There are currently no qualified exemptions to Measure H, and there is no application form available.”
0
Zero seniors have ever qualified for a senior exemption from El Cerrito’s existing Measure H parcel tax — which uses the identical exemption language the backers have now copied into Measure C. Source: El Cerrito Finance Director Crystal Reams, via Livable El Cerrito.

The backers did not stumble into this language by accident. It is already in the El Cerrito Municipal Code. They copied it knowing full well how it works in practice. If Measure C passes, the senior exemption will produce the same result it has produced for Measure H: nothing.

6. No application exists

The city does not have an application form for the existing exemption

Measure C says seniors “must apply” for the exemption and that the Tax Administrator shall provide forms. For the existing Measure H senior exemption — which has used the same language for years — the city has never created one.

This was confirmed by the Tax Administrator herself. In the same Feb. 10 email in which she told Livable El Cerrito that no seniors have qualified, Finance Director Crystal Reams stated directly that “there is no application form available.”

She also explained what a senior would have to do if they wanted to attempt to qualify under Measure H today — and what they would have to attach to that submission:

Crystal Reams, Finance Director and Tax Administrator, City of El Cerrito
via Livable El Cerrito, Feb. 10, 2026
To qualify, one of the owners must qualify for participation in either the Gonzales-Deukmejian-Petris Senior Citizen Property Tax Assistance Law or the Senior Citizen Property Tax Postponement Law. “Proof from the State Controller’s Office or Franchise Tax Board is required.” The property owner must submit a letter to the Tax Administrator stating they would like to apply for the exemption and include the proof from the state.

In other words: the only way to get the exemption is to produce a document proving enrollment in a state program that either no longer exists or requires a lien on the home. The city has had years to build an application process for Measure H and declined to do so. There is no credible reason to believe Measure C will be any different.

7. This was a choice

Other local measures exempt any homeowner 65 and older. This one does not.

Defenders of Measure C have argued the exemption language is standard boilerplate. It is not. Other parcel taxes in the area exempt any homeowner 65 or older — full stop. No state program required. No income test. No lien. The clearest local example is the West Contra Costa Unified School District’s Measure T, the parcel tax that funds local schools.

Measure T’s exemption language, quoted in full by Livable El Cerrito, reads:

West Contra Costa Unified School District Measure T
Senior Exemption Language
“An exemption shall be granted annually on any single-family residential parcel or multi-family dwelling unit owned by one or more persons 65 years of age or older who occupies said parcel or unit as a principal residence, upon annual application to the District for exemption.”

That is what a real senior exemption looks like: age 65, own it, live in it, apply. Done.

Measure T (School District)

A senior exemption that works.

Any homeowner 65 or older who owns and occupies a single-family residence or multi-family dwelling unit is exempt on annual application. No state program enrollment required. No income test. No lien.

Seniors who apply, qualify.

Measure C (Library Tax)

A senior exemption that doesn’t.

Exemption requires proof of enrollment in a defunct state grant program or the Property Tax Postponement program — which imposes an income cap of roughly $55,000, a 40% equity floor, a ban on reverse mortgages, and a state lien on the home.

Zero seniors have qualified under identical language in Measure H.

The backers of Measure C know Measure T exists. They live in the same school district. Measure T’s exemption language was available to copy. It was not copied.

“If they had wanted a senior exemption that worked, they would have written one. They didn’t.”
8. Renters

Senior renters get nothing

A significant share of El Cerrito households rent their homes, and many are older residents on fixed incomes. The tax is levied on improvements — square footage of buildings — so landlords pay it on every unit in every apartment building in the city. Those costs get passed through in rent. It is a basic feature of how parcel taxes on rental property work.

The exemption language in Measure C does not reach renters at all. Both referenced state programs are tied to homeownership. A 78-year-old living on Social Security in an apartment on San Pablo Avenue has no path to an exemption, even in theory, even under the measure’s most generous reading.

A senior exemption that exempts no one is not a senior exemption.
It is a talking point printed on the ballot to make a tax easier to pass.
